PC1 feature loadings — what drives the first principal component

PC1 captures 37% of total variance. Features with large positive loadings (high credit score, more products, longer tenure) push customers toward the retained end. Features with large negative loadings (high complaints, months since contact, low app logins) push toward the churn end.

Scenario: A mid-tier Nigerian commercial bank (Lagos HQ) collects 10 behavioural features per customer monthly - credit score, account tenure, transaction frequency, balance, app logins, complaints, and more. With 10 dimensions, it's impossible to visually spot which customers are drifting toward churn. Dimensionality reduction compresses those 10 features into 2, making churn risk visible to relationship managers without any ML expertise.

The Python application covers:

  1. PCA - linear, fast, interpretable. PC1 alone captures 37% of variance. The scree plot (tab 3) shows that 3 components retain ~57% of total variance. The feature loadings tab shows exactly which features drive PC1: high credit score, more products, and longer tenure → retained; high complaints and months since last contact → churn
  2. t-SNE - non-linear, reveals local cluster shape PCA misses. KL divergence = 1.308 (good fit). Churned customers form a tighter, more isolated cluster
  3. Comparison - silhouette score used to objectively compare the two projections in 2-D (PCA = 0.661 vs t-SNE = 0.656 - PCA wins marginally on this data)

Key design decisions documented in code:

  1. StandardScaler applied before PCA - mandatory because credit score (300-850) would dominate Euclidean distances over loan count (0-3) without it
  2. t-SNE uses init='pca' - more stable than random initialisation for this sample size
  3. Both techniques compared quantitatively with silhouette score, not just visually
